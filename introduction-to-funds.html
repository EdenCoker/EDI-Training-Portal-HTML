History of Funds

​The history of funds dates back to the 18th century, specifically in 1602, when the Dutch East India Company issued the first publicly traded shares. However, the modern fund industry, as we know it today, emerged in the early 20th century with the introduction of the mutual fund.In 1924, the Massachusetts Investors Trust, the first open-end mutual fund, was launched in the United States. This marked a significant milestone in the development of funds. 

The concept behind mutual funds involved pooling money from multiple investors to create a diversified portfolio of securities. This approach allowed investors with limited capital to access a broader range of investment opportunities.The mutual fund industry experienced rapid growth in the following decades as more investors recognised the benefits of this investment vehicle. By pooling resources together, individuals could achieve greater diversification and have their investments professionally managed by fund managers. This allowed investors to participate in various asset classes, including stocks, bonds, and other securities, without having to individually select and manage each investment.

In the 1990s, a new type of fund called exchange-traded funds (ETFs) was introduced in Canada. ETFs provided investors with a more flexible and cost-effective way to invest in a diversified portfolio of securities. Like mutual funds, ETFs pool money from multiple investors, but they differ in the way they are bought and sold on stock exchanges. ETFs can be traded throughout the day, similar to individual stocks, whereas mutual funds are typically bought and sold at the end of the trading day at the net asset value (NAV) price.The introduction of ETFs expanded the options available to investors, providing them with greater flexibility and control over their investment strategies. 

ETFs gained popularity in the 1990s and 2000s, offering advantages such as lower expense ratios, tax efficiency, and the ability to trade intraday. Today, ETFs have become a major segment of the fund industry, with a wide range of options available to investors seeking diversified investment exposure across various asset classes, sectors, and regions.To summarise, the history of funds can be traced back to the 18th century, but the modern fund industry emerged in the early 20th century with the mutual fund. The introduction of mutual funds allowed investors to pool their money and access diversified portfolios managed by professionals. 

The subsequent development of ETFs in the 1990s provided investors with additional choices and advantages in terms of flexibility and cost-effectiveness. Together, mutual funds and ETFs have revolutionised the way individuals invest and have become integral components of the fund industry.

What is a Fund

​A fund is a financial vehicle that allows multiple investors to pool their money together to create a diversified portfolio of assets. These assets can include stocks, bonds, or other types of securities. The fund is overseen and managed by a professional investment manager or team who are responsible for making investment decisions on behalf of the investors.

The primary objective of a fund is to generate a return on investment for the investors. This return can be realised through various means, such as capital gains from the appreciation in the value of the fund's assets, dividends received from the underlying securities, or interest payments from bonds or other fixed-income instruments held by the fund.

There are different types of funds available to investors, each with its own structure and characteristics. Some common types include open-end funds, closed-end funds, and exchange-traded funds (ETFs).

Open-end funds are the most prevalent type of fund. These funds issue an unlimited number of shares to investors and are continuously open for new investments and redemptions. Investors can buy and sell shares directly from the fund company at the net asset value (NAV) price, which represents the value of the fund's underlying assets divided by the number of shares outstanding.

Closed-end funds, on the other hand, have a fixed number of shares that are typically bought and sold on a stock exchange. Unlike open-end funds, closed-end funds do not issue or redeem shares on a daily basis. Instead, investors can buy or sell shares from other investors in the secondary market, and the price of the shares may trade at a premium or discount to the net asset value.

Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are a type of fund that combines features of both open-end and closed-end funds. Like closed-end funds, ETFs trade on stock exchanges, and their shares can be bought and sold throughout the trading day at market prices. However, similar to open-end funds, ETFs can create and redeem shares to meet investor demand, helping to keep their share prices in line with the net asset value.

Investors can participate in funds by buying shares, which represent a proportional ownership interest in the fund's assets. These shares can be purchased either directly from the fund company or through a broker on an exchange. When an investor buys shares, their money is added to the fund's pool of assets. Conversely, when an investor sells shares, they receive the corresponding value based on the current share price.
In summary, funds are investment vehicles that enable individuals to pool their money together to create diversified portfolios managed by professionals. These portfolios can consist of various assets, and the objective is to generate a return on investment for the investors. Funds can be structured as open-end funds, closed-end funds, or ETFs, offering investors different options for buying and selling shares. By investing in funds, individuals can access a broader range of investment opportunities and benefit from professional management expertise. 

​Involved Parties in a Fund

The fund industry comprises several key participants who play different roles in the creation, management, distribution, and regulation of investment funds.


Fund issuers - Typically asset management companies or financial institutions responsible for creating and managing investment funds. They design the fund's investment strategy, select the underlying assets, and handle the day-to-day operations of the fund. These issuers make decisions on portfolio composition, asset allocation, and investment tactics based on the fund's objectives. Examples of fund issuers include companies like BlackRock, Vanguard, and Fidelity Investments, which offer a wide range of investment funds catering to different investor needs. Investors can range from individual retail investors to pension funds, endowments, and insurance companies.

Investors - Individuals or institutions that provide the capital invested in the fund. Investors have various objectives, such as diversification, income generation, capital appreciation, or a combination of these. They buy shares in the fund, effectively becoming shareholders and participating in the fund's performance. Investors can enter or exit a fund by buying or selling fund shares, and they may receive periodic distributions such as dividends, capital gains, or interest payments from the fund.

Fund administrators - Third-party service providers that handle the operational tasks of the fund. They ensure accurate accounting, reporting, and compliance with regulatory requirements. Fund administrators manage the fund's books and records, calculate net asset value (NAV), prepare financial statements, and provide timely reports to investors. They play a crucial role in maintaining transparency and operational efficiency for the fund. Fund administrators such as State Street Corporation and Northern Trust provide back-office services to funds, ensuring accurate record-keeping and reporting.

Distributors - Firms involved in marketing and selling fund shares to investors. They may operate directly or through intermediaries like financial advisors, banks, or online platforms. Distributors help investors understand the features, risks, and potential benefits of different funds. They may provide educational materials, support, and guidance to investors to help them make informed investment decisions. Distributors may include firms like Charles Schwab or TD Ameritrade that offer online platforms for buying and selling fund shares.

Regulators - Government agencies responsible for overseeing the fund industry and ensuring compliance with laws and regulations. They establish rules and standards for fund issuers, distributors, and administrators to safeguard investor interests and maintain market integrity. Regulators monitor the fund industry, conduct examinations, and enforce compliance to protect investors from fraudulent activities and ensure fair practises. Regulators can vary by jurisdiction but may include agencies like the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) or the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in the United Kingdom.

Inception Stage

Fund Creation and Legal Structure

The inception stage marks the birth of a fund and involves critical decisions regarding its structure and legal framework.


Here's a detailed look at the key aspects:
Fund Entity Establishment: Before a fund can operate, it must be legally established. This typically involves setting up an entity, such as a mutual fund, exchange-traded fund (ETF), hedge fund, or private equity fund. The choice of entity impacts taxation, regulatory oversight, and fund structure.

Legal Structure Selection: Fund managers must carefully choose the legal structure that best aligns with their objectives and investor base. Common structures include open-end funds, closed-end funds, and unit investment trusts. The choice influences liquidity, trading, and governance.Documentation and Bylaws: Creating the legal framework for the fund involves drafting the fund's governing documents and bylaws. These documents outline the fund's purpose, rules, investment strategies, and operating procedures. They provide the legal foundation for the fund's operations.Regulatory Compliance: During the fund creation process, it is essential to comply with local and international regulations. This includes registering with relevant regulatory authorities, such as the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) for U.S.-based funds, and adhering to the rules and guidelines specific to the fund type and jurisdiction.






Fund Documentation and Offering

Once the fund entity is established and the legal structure is in place, the next step is to create the fund documentation and offer the investment opportunity to potential investors:



Prospectus Preparation : The prospectus is a comprehensive document that provides detailed information about the fund. It includes investment objectives, strategies, risks, fees, historical performance, and legal terms. The prospectus is a crucial resource for investors to make informed decisions.



Offering to Potential Investors : Fund managers initiate the process of attracting investors by offering shares or units of the fund. This offering typically includes a subscription process where investors can purchase shares and become shareholders of the fund.



Sales and Marketing Materials : In addition to the prospectus, marketing materials are created to promote the fund to potential investors. These materials may include brochures, presentations, and digital marketing campaigns, all designed to articulate the fund's value proposition.



Compliance with Marketing Regulations : It is imperative to comply with marketing and advertising regulations, which vary by jurisdiction. Fund managers need to ensure that their marketing materials are accurate, not misleading, and adhere to legal requirements.



Capital Raising Stage

The capital raising stage is where fund managers actively seek investors and manage their subscriptions and redemptions. This stage is crucial for the growth and sustainability of the fund.



Marketing and Distribution

Fund Promotion : Effective marketing is essential to attract investors. Marketing efforts may include presenting the fund's historical performance, unique selling points, and the expertise of the fund management team.



Distribution Channels : Fund managers need to identify appropriate distribution channels to reach their target audience. This can involve working with financial advisors, broker-dealers, or directly marketing to individual investors.



Regulatory Considerations : Marketing and distribution activities should comply with regulations specific to the fund and its jurisdiction. Understanding the regulatory landscape is vital to avoid legal issues.



Subscription and Redemption

Investor Subscriptions : During this stage, investors subscribe to the fund by purchasing shares or units. Subscription processes may vary, and some funds offer both periodic subscriptions and continuous subscriptions, allowing investors to enter or exit at their convenience.



Redemptions : Redemptions are requests from investors to sell their shares or units in the fund. Managing redemptions, especially in open-end funds, requires maintaining liquidity and ensuring that redemption requests are fulfilled in accordance with the fund's rules.



Subscription and Redemption Pricing : Pricing shares for new subscriptions and redemptions is a complex process that depends on the fund's net asset value (NAV) and the timing of the transaction.



Investment and Portfolio Management

Asset Allocation

Strategic Asset Allocation : In this stage, fund managers decide on the long-term allocation of the fund's assets among different asset classes, such as equities, fixed income, real estate, and alternative investments. The goal is to align the fund's allocation with its objectives and risk tolerance.



Tactical Asset Allocation : Fund managers may also make tactical shifts in response to changing market conditions or opportunities within asset classes. This involves periodic rebalancing to maintain the desired risk-return profile.



Investment Strategy

Defining the Investment Approach : Fund managers must articulate the fund's investment approach, which could be passive indexing, active management, quantitative strategies, or alternative investment strategies. This decision impacts how the fund's assets are managed and can affect performance and fees.



Portfolio Construction : Fund managers construct the fund's portfolio by selecting specific securities or assets that align with the chosen investment strategy. Diversification, risk management, and performance targets are critical considerations.



Reporting and Compliance

Regulatory Filings

Legal Obligations : Fund managers must adhere to regulatory requirements for periodic reporting, which typically includes disclosures of fund holdings, performance, fees, and other relevant information.



Form Filing : In the United States, mutual funds must file Form N-1A with the SEC, while other funds have different filing requirements. These filings ensure transparency and regulatory compliance.



Periodic Reporting to Investors

Investor Communication : Fund managers provide regular reports to investors, offering insights into the fund's performance, portfolio holdings, and changes in its strategies. This helps investors make informed decisions about their investments.



Performance Measurement : Assessing the fund's performance against its benchmark and objectives is a critical aspect of reporting. Metrics such as total return, risk-adjusted performance, and attribution analysis are commonly used.



Liquidation Stage

Reasons for Liquidation

Poor Performance : If a fund consistently underperforms and fails to meet its investment objectives, fund managers may choose to liquidate the fund to protect investors from further losses.



Achievement of Objectives : In some cases, funds have specific objectives, such as reaching a certain size, duration, or investment goal. Once these objectives are met, fund managers may opt to liquidate the fund.



Regulatory or Legal Changes : Changes in regulations or tax laws may prompt fund managers to liquidate a fund, especially if it no longer aligns with the new regulatory landscape.



Distribution of Proceeds

Asset Liquidation : Upon deciding to liquidate the fund, the fund manager initiates the process of selling the fund's assets. This can be a complex process, and it's crucial to maximize the value of the assets.



Proceeds Distribution : After selling the assets, the fund distributes the proceeds to shareholders. The distribution may involve one-time payments or periodic payouts, depending on the fund's structure and terms.



In conclusion, understanding the inception and life cycle stages of a fund is essential for fund managers and investors alike. Effective fund creation, management, reporting, and, when necessary, liquidation are critical components in delivering value to investors and achieving fund objectives. Fund managers must navigate these stages carefully to ensure regulatory compliance, transparency, and investor trust.
