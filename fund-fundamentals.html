Issuer Level Data

Issuer level data in the fund industry encompasses various pieces of information related to the company or institution that issues and manages a particular fund. This data provides valuable insights for investors and other stakeholders. Here are some examples of issuer level data:

1. Name and Contact Information of the Fund Issuer:
- The name and legal entity of the company or institution responsible for creating and managing the fund.
- Contact details, including address, phone number, and website, through which investors can reach the fund issuer for enquiries or additional information.

2. Fund Strategy and Investment Objectives:
- A description of the investment strategy employed by the fund, outlining the approach taken to select and manage the fund's assets.
- Clear articulation of the fund's investment objectives, such as capital appreciation, income generation, or a combination of both.

3. Fund Performance Data:
- Historical performance metrics that provide insight into the fund's past returns, such as annualised returns over different time periods.
- Fee structure and expense ratios that indicate the costs associated with investing in the fund.

4. Legal and Regulatory Information:
- Registration status of the fund, confirming that it complies with applicable regulations and has met the necessary licencing requirements.
- Regulatory disclosures and filings that offer transparency into the fund's legal and compliance history, including any disciplinary actions or violations.

5. Asset Allocation and Investment Holdings:
- Details about the fund's asset allocation, which indicates the proportion of the portfolio invested in various asset classes such as stocks, bonds, or alternative investments.
- Information on specific investments or holdings within the fund, providing insight into the underlying securities or assets in which the fund is invested.

6. Fund Management Team Information:
- Names and qualifications of key personnel involved in managing the fund, including portfolio managers, analysts, and other investment professionals.
- Relevant experience and track record of the fund management team, showcasing their expertise and ability to make investment decisions on behalf of the fund.


Examples of issuer level data can be found in the fund prospectus, annual reports, fact sheets, and other documents provided by the fund issuer. This information helps investors evaluate the fund's suitability, understand its investment strategy, assess past performance, and make informed investment decisions. It also assists regulatory bodies in overseeing the fund industry and ensuring transparency and compliance with applicable laws and regulations.

Sub Fund Level Data

Sub-fund level data refers to the specific information related to each sub-fund within a fund structure. This data provides insights into the characteristics and performance of individual sub-funds. Here are some examples of sub-fund level data:


Sub-fund Name: Each sub-fund within a larger fund structure typically has a distinct name that distinguishes it from other sub-funds.

Sub-fund Identifier Code: An identifier code unique to each sub-fund that helps identify and track it within the fund structure.

Investment Objective and Strategy: The specific goals and investment approach of the sub-fund, outlining the intended outcomes and the methodology used to achieve them.

Benchmark Index: A benchmark index against which the performance of the sub-fund is measured, providing a reference point for evaluating its relative performance.

Geographic and Sector Allocation: Information about the geographic regions or countries in which the sub-fund invests, as well as the sectors or industries it focuses on.

Asset Allocation and Diversification Targets: Details about the allocation of assets within the sub-fund, indicating the proportion of investments in different asset classes such as stocks, bonds, or commodities. Diversification targets specifying the desired level of diversification across different investments within the sub-fund.

Management Fees and Expenses: The fees and expenses associated with managing the sub-fund, including management fees, administrative costs, and other charges incurred by investors.

Performance History and Risk Measures: Historical performance data for the sub-fund, such as returns over different time periods, allowing investors to assess its past performance. Risk measures, such as standard deviation or beta, that provide insights into the volatility and potential risks associated with the sub-fund.


Sub-fund level data is crucial for investors to evaluate the performance and characteristics of each sub-fund within a larger fund structure. It helps investors compare sub-funds, understand their investment strategies, assess risk levels, and make informed investment decisions based on their investment objectives and preferences.

Fund managers also rely on sub-fund level data to monitor and optimise the performance and risk profile of each sub-fund. By analysing the data, fund managers can identify areas of strength or weakness, rebalance asset allocations, and make strategic adjustments to meet the evolving needs of investors and adapt to changing market conditions.

Fund Share Class Data

Fund share class data refers to the various classes of shares available within a single fund, each with its own characteristics, fee structures, and suitability for different types of investors. Understanding the distinctions between share classes is important for investors to make informed decisions. Here are some examples of fund share classes:


1. Institutional Share Class:
- These shares are typically designed for institutional investors, such as pension funds, endowments, or large corporations.
- Institutional share classes often have lower expense ratios compared to other share classes, as they cater to investors with substantial investment amounts.
- They may also have lower minimum investment requirements, allowing institutions to access the fund with significant investments.

2. Retail Share Class:
- Retail share classes are targeted towards individual investors.
- They may have higher expense ratios compared to institutional share classes, as they accommodate smaller investment amounts.
- Retail share classes often have lower minimum investment requirements, making them accessible to a broader range of individual investors.

3. Advisor Share Class:
- Advisor share classes are typically available through financial advisors or registered investment advisors.
- They generally have expense ratios higher than institutional share classes but lower than retail share classes.
- Advisor share classes often provide compensation to financial advisors through trailing commissions or 12b-1 fees.

4. Class A, B, and C Shares:
- These share classes offer different fee structures and sales loads.
- Class A shares typically have lower upfront fees or sales charges but may have higher ongoing expenses.
- Class B shares may have higher upfront fees but lower ongoing expenses. They may also have contingent deferred sales charges (CDSC) if shares are redeemed early.
- Class C shares may have higher ongoing expenses and no upfront sales charges. They may have lower CDSC than Class B shares.

5. Load vs. No-Load Share Classes:
- Load share classes charge a sales commission or load fee when shares are bought (front-end load) or sold (back-end load).
- No-load share classes do not impose sales commissions or load fees, making them more cost-effective for investors.


Fund share class data may also include information on the performance track record, liquidity, and investment strategy associated with each share class. This data helps investors compare share classes, assess their suitability based on investment goals and preferences, and evaluate the potential costs and benefits associated with each class.

What are dividends

Dividends are a portion of a company or fund's profits distributed to its shareholders. In the case of a company, these profits could alternatively be reinvested in the business instead of being distributed. However, for funds, all dividends, interest earned, and in the case of property funds, rental income, must be distributed to its investors.

Dividends are typically paid out in cash but can also be reinvested in the fund. Funds that pay cash are known as income funds, and those where the income is reinvested are termed accumulation funds.

In the UK, dividends are subject to specific rules and regulations. For example, different tax rates apply to dividends depending on the investor's income tax bracket.

Dividends from UK sources were, until 2016, subject to a Dividend Tax Credit, which served to decrease tax liability. However, this was replaced by the Dividend Allowance in 2016. As of 2021, the tax-free Dividend Allowance was set at £2,000, after which taxes apply based on the taxpayer's income tax band. Dividends, whether they're from the UK or abroad, are taxed at different rates: 7.5% for basic rate taxpayers, 32.5% for higher rate taxpayers, and 38.1% for additional rate taxpayers. It's advisable to consult a tax advisor or financial planner for the most accurate and up-to-date advice due to the complexity and fluid nature of tax laws.

Let's illustrate this with an example. If a UK company pays a dividend of £1 per share and an investor owns 100 shares, they would receive a dividend payment of £100. The taxation on this dividend would depend on the investor's income tax band and whether the total dividends received in that tax year exceed the Dividend Allowance of £2,000.

Dividends can be a significant source of income for investors, and understanding the rules and regulations around dividends in your specific region or country is an important consideration when making investment decisions.

Equalisation is a process used in investment funds to ensure a fair distribution of income among all shareholders, irrespective of when they purchased their shares within a distribution period. This is particularly relevant for funds that pay dividends or distribute interest. When an investor buys shares in a fund, part of the purchase price may include some accrued income from the fund's investments that has not yet been paid out. If a distribution is made soon after, this income is returned to the investor as 'equalisation'. This equalisation payment is considered a return of capital, not income, meaning it is not subject to income tax. It ensures that all shareholders are treated equitably, regardless of when they entered the fund during a distribution period.
